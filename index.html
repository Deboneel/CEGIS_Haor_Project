00 Introduction

The Haor Region sits in northeastern Bangladesh — mostly Sylhet and Sunamganj — where the land spends half the year underwater. One boro rice crop in the dry months before the water comes back.

This exercise maps six physical characteristics of the region in ArcGIS Pro: agro-ecological zones, land types, soil texture, drainage patterns, surface water recession timing, and available soil moisture. Taken together, they describe how land and water shape what is agronomically possible here.

The maps were prepared using shapefiles from soil and land-use surveys. A critical preprocessing step was resolving a missing coordinate system in the soil shapefile — Define Projection was applied using the Gulshan_303_Bangladesh_TM coordinate system, which uses the D Gulshan 303 datum and Transverse Mercator projection.

Data Preparation

Both shapefiles were examined for attribute table quality and missing values before mapping. Missing or inconsistent values were assigned a "No Data" category to maintain completeness across all map outputs.

Fig. A Agro-ecological Zones

Where Floods Define the Farming Calendar

1,944,041 ha across 11 agro-ecological zones from piedmont to deep floodplain basin

The east is dominated by Sylhet Basin and Eastern Surma-Kusiyara Floodplain — both deep-flooding zones suited to boro rice only. The west transitions to Old Brahmaputra Floodplain with moderate flooding and broader crop options. Zone distribution directly determines flood depth, soil type, and cropping potential across the region.

Sylhet Basin 22.9% E. Surma-Kusiyara 22.7% N. & E. Piedmont 14.2% Old Meghna Estuarine 12.3%

Fig. B Land Type Classification

From Highland Fringe to the Deepest Basin Floor

1,969,897 ha across 8 land type categories — Lowland is the largest single class at 22.2%

Lowland (437,921 ha, 22.2%) spreads across Sunamganj, Habiganj, and Sylhet. Very Lowland (215,280 ha, 10.9%) marks the deepest flood-prone areas in the core haor basin — the last areas to drain and the first to flood. Only Highland areas (14.5%) along the peripheral fringes offer crop diversification beyond boro rice.

Lowland 22.2% Medium Highland 21.6% Medium Lowland 18.8% Very Lowland 10.9%

Fig. C Top Soil Texture

68% Clay or Clay Loam — the Soil That Holds Water

39.2% Clay Loam — the dominant texture (771,935 ha), poorly drained and water-retentive

Clay (29.0%, 571,447 ha) concentrates in the deep haor areas of Sunamganj and Habiganj. Together, Clay Loam and Clay cover over 68% of the region — both are poorly drained, water-retentive soils that flood easily and dry slowly, fundamentally shaping what crops are viable. Loam (18.1%) provides better drainage in western and peripheral zones.

Clay Loam 39.2% Clay 29.0% Loam 18.1% Settlement 7.7%

Fig. D Drainage Characteristics

Over 46% of Land Is Poorly or Very Poorly Drained

41.4% Poorly Drained — covering 1,087,214 ha across the central basin

Poorly Drained land covers most of the central basin across Sunamganj, Habiganj, and Kishoreganj. Char (25.1%, 659,031 ha) represents low-lying river deposits with seasonal inundation. Together, poorly and very poorly drained soils (46.9%) set the fundamental constraint on agricultural planning — they are the single largest reason why the Haor Region cannot diversify beyond boro rice at scale.

Poorly Drained 41.4% Char 25.1% Imperfectly Drained 13.0% Very Poorly Drained 5.5%

Fig. E Surface Water Recession

Late Recession Governs the Agricultural Window

26.0% Late recession — water clears into the dry season in Sunamganj and Habiganj cores

Extremely Early recession (22.5%, 443,865 ha) occurs along outer fringes and elevated areas where water drains quickly after monsoon. Very Late (7.6%, 149,565 ha) marks the last areas to dry — the deep basin cores of Sunamganj. Recession timing directly determines the planting window; late and very late classes collectively narrow viable cropping periods to a tight window of boro rice.

Fig. F Organic Matter / Soil Moisture

Dry Season Moisture Runs Out Where It Matters Most

41.1% Low moisture — northern and eastern fringes drain early and dry out fastest

Only three classes are needed to describe moisture across 1,969,898 ha. Medium moisture (29.3%, 577,304 ha) sits in the transitional zones of Habiganj and Kishoreganj. High moisture (10.9%, 213,972 ha) covers the least area but occupies exactly the right position — the deep basin of Sunamganj, where waterlogging outlasts every other condition on the map.

Putting the six layers together

Water Controls Everything Here

Stack the six layers and one picture emerges. The soils are fine-textured and slow to drain. Water recedes late across the core basin. Dry-season moisture runs out fast almost everywhere except the deepest ground, where it barely runs out at all. Over 46% of the region is poorly or very poorly drained, and that single number explains most of what farmers here can and cannot do.

The AEZ and Land Type maps confirm this from a different angle — more than half the Haor sits in lowland or very lowland categories, where flood depth and duration leave no room for a second crop. Only the highland and piedmont edges — ground that drains fast and dries early — get more than one shot at the calendar.

Flood management, land use planning, and crop scheduling in the Haor all run into the same constraint eventually. The water decides.

Technical Context

This work was carried out as Exercise 01 of the CEGIS ArcGIS Pro tutorial series, focusing on thematic map preparation techniques for the Haor Region of Bangladesh. The exercise covers the complete workflow from raw shapefile preprocessing through final map layout production.

Key skills demonstrated include coordinate system correction with Define Projection, geoprocessing with Dissolve and Clip, attribute cleaning with Field Calculator Python expressions, Calculate Geometry for area statistics, and professional cartographic layout design.
